# Tropical North Queensland

## Reef and islands

Port Douglas is a launch point for the northern Great Barrier Reef, with day
boats running to the outer reef and to the Low Isles for snorkelling and diving.
Operators supply gear and stinger suits, and calmer inner-reef trips suit
first-timers and families. Book ahead in peak season and choose a smaller boat for
quieter sites. Reef trips run most days but can be cancelled in strong wind, so
keep the itinerary flexible.

## Daintree and rainforest

Inland and north, the Daintree is one of the oldest rainforests on earth, reached
by the Daintree River ferry and running up to Cape Tribulation where the forest
meets the reef. Boardwalks at Mossman Gorge and Marrdja let you walk the canopy
and creeks without a guide, and river cruises look for saltwater crocodiles.
Roads north of the Daintree ferry are narrow and partly unsealed — check whether
your rig is suited before committing.

## Town, food and markets

Port Douglas itself is a compact resort town: Macrossan Street's restaurants and
bars, the Sunday markets by the waterfront, and Four Mile Beach for a long flat
walk. Nearby Mossman and the cane country give a quieter, working-town contrast.
The tropical produce — mangoes, tropical fruit, seafood — is a highlight of the
markets and menus through the dry season.

## Wet-weather and stinger-season notes

The wet season runs roughly November to April, bringing heavy afternoon downpours,
heat and humidity, and marine stingers in the ocean — swim in netted enclosures or
stinger suits and heed local signs. Rainy days suit a rainforest boardwalk under
the canopy, a river cruise, or the covered markets and cafes in town. The dry
season, May to October, is the reliable window for reef and beach days.

## Campgrounds and facilities

Holiday parks sit around Port Douglas, Mossman and along the Captain Cook Highway,
with powered sites, camp kitchens, dump points and laundries; some have pools,
welcome in the heat. Book ahead for the dry-season peak. Beyond the Daintree
ferry, facilities thin out and sites are basic, so stock up on fuel, water and
supplies before crossing. Check clearance and road conditions for any unsealed
sections.

## Local tips and FAQs

The dry season is the comfortable time to visit; the wet is hot, humid and
storm-prone. Never swim outside netted areas in stinger season, and never enter
rivers or estuaries where crocodiles live — obey the warning signs. The tropical
sun is intense; carry water, shade and reef-safe sunscreen. Book reef trips and
Daintree tours ahead in peak weeks, and keep plans flexible around weather.
