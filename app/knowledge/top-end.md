# Top End

## Darwin and the waterfront

Darwin is the Top End's hub and supply town — fuel, groceries and repairs before
any bush leg. The Waterfront Precinct has a netted swimming lagoon and wave pool
(safe from crocodiles and stingers), the Mindil Beach Sunset Market runs in the
dry season for food stalls and crafts, and the Museum and Art Gallery of the
Northern Territory covers the region's art and the story of Cyclone Tracy. Sunsets
over the harbour are a nightly event.

## Kakadu and Litchfield

Two great national parks flank the city. Litchfield, about ninety minutes south,
has accessible waterfalls and clear plunge pools at Wangi, Florence and Buley that
are the easiest swimming in the Top End. Kakadu, further east, is vast — rock art
at Ubirr and Nourlangie, wetlands alive with birds, and cruises on the Yellow
Water billabong. Distances and heat are serious, so plan fuel, water and driving
times with room to spare.

## Waterholes, swimming and crocodile safety

Swimming here is only ever where it is signed as safe. Both freshwater and
dangerous saltwater crocodiles live across the region, so never enter rivers,
billabongs or estuaries unless a plunge pool is explicitly marked safe and checked
for the season. Litchfield's monitored pools and Darwin's netted lagoon are the
reliable options. Marine stingers add risk to the ocean in the wet — treat any
unsigned water as off-limits.

## Seasons: the Dry and the Wet

The Top End runs on two seasons. The Dry (roughly May to October) is warm, clear
and the main visiting window, with waterfalls and roads open. The Wet (November to
April) brings heavy monsoon storms, extreme humidity, road closures from flooding,
and spectacular lightning; some parks and tracks close entirely. On a wet day the
Darwin museums, the Waterfront and a wildlife park make a comfortable indoor plan.

## Campgrounds and facilities

Holiday parks around Darwin and Litchfield have powered sites, camp kitchens, dump
points, laundries and pools — pools matter in this heat. Inside Kakadu, campgrounds
range from serviced to bush-basic, so carry water, fuel and supplies for remote
legs. Book Dry-season sites ahead. Some access roads flood or turn to dirt in the
Wet, so check conditions and whether your rig suits before leaving the highway.

## Local tips and FAQs

Visit in the Dry for open roads and safe swimming; the Wet is for storm-chasers and
seasoned travellers. Assume all natural water holds crocodiles unless a sign and the
season say otherwise — this is the single most important rule up here. Heat and
humidity are extreme, so carry and drink far more water than usual and rest in the
midday heat. Fuel up at every town, and book Dry-season campgrounds and tours ahead.
